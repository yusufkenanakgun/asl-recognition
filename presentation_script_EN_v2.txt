========================================================
SLIDE 1 — COVER  (~30 seconds)
========================================================

Good morning. My name is Yusuf Kenan Akgün, and today I'm presenting my
senior project: an on-device American Sign Language recognition system.

The core idea is in the subtitle — I built a sign language recognizer
that runs entirely on a phone, and I used it to compare two approaches:
one based on raw camera images, and one based on hand landmarks.

[gesture to the screenshots on the right]

What you see on the right is the actual application running on a real
phone, recognizing the letters A, B and X in real time. So everything I
describe today is a working app, not a simulation.


========================================================
SLIDE 2 — INTRODUCTION  (~75 seconds)
========================================================

Let me start with the problem, because it shaped every decision.

Sign language is the everyday language of tens of millions of people,
but it's still underserved by consumer technology. Voice assistants,
captions, translation tools — they all assume audio input by default,
which excludes sign language users. So visual recognition models become
necessary.

There is good research on this. But here is the gap I built my project
around: the literature optimizes offline accuracy — accuracy on a
powerful server — and rarely asks about deployability. In other words,
the accuracy is measured on a server, not on a consumer phone. Being
accurate on a server is not the same as being usable on a phone, and
that distinction is the heart of my thesis.

I addressed two complementary tasks. The first is letters —
fingerspelling — 29 static classes: the 26 ASL letters plus three
auxiliary classes, delete, space, and nothing. The second is words. I
use only the WLASL-100 benchmark; the larger 300, 1000 and 2000 subsets
are out of scope.

And the one hard constraint across both tasks: everything runs one
hundred percent on the device. No server.

[point to the flow on the right]

On the right you see the recognition flow for the sign "drink" — three
steps: Collect, Ready, Result. The app collects a 32-frame window and
the BiLSTM latches the top prediction.


========================================================
SLIDE 3 — SYSTEM ARCHITECTURE  (~55 seconds)
========================================================

Architecturally, the system is two separate worlds connected by one
artefact.

[trace left to right]

On the left is the offline world — PyTorch, on my computer. You see the
two datasets, and the four models I train: EfficientNet-B0, the landmark
MLP, the single BiLSTM, and the five-seed ensemble.

On the right is the device world — Android, in Kotlin. CameraX feeds
frames, MediaPipe extracts the landmarks, the Inference Manager runs the
active model, and the UI in Compose shows the result.

The bridge between these two worlds is TensorFlow Lite. I train in
PyTorch, convert to TFLite, and that single converted checkpoint is what
actually runs on the phone. One artefact bridges two worlds — four
models, two datasets, one Android app.


========================================================
SLIDE 4 — IMPLEMENTATION  (~80 seconds)
========================================================

A little detail on how this was built.

[point to the models]

On the letters task there are two models: EfficientNet-B0, which is an
image-based model on 224 RGB input with ImageNet pretraining, and the
landmark MLP, which is a landmark-based model on the 63-dimensional
MediaPipe pose. On the words task, the BiLSTM, which also uses
landmarks — a two-hand stream — and the five-seed ensemble on top.

[point to the datasets]

Two datasets. For letters, the ASL Alphabet — 87,000 images. For words,
WLASL-100, and here I had a real problem: many of the original videos
are link-rotted in 2026. The Kaggle bundle alone gave me about a
thousand clips. So I wrote a recovery pipeline with yt-dlp that pulled
back more videos from sign-language dictionary sites, taking me to 1,465
clips — a 44 percent increase.

[point to the ablation pillars]

Now, my word model did not start at 60 percent — it started at 20. I
improved it through a forward-additive ablation, where I isolate each
change to measure its own gain, grouped into four pillars. First, data
engineering — the recovery I just mentioned, plus re-balancing. Second,
extraction — moving from one hand to two, with hand-crop normalization.
Third, the training recipe — augmentation, mixup, and a weighted
sampler. And fourth, the five-seed ensemble.

[emphasize]

The key finding: data engineering was the single largest lever — about
sixteen points on its own. On this dataset, more data beat every
training trick I tried.


========================================================
SLIDE 5 — TESTS & RESULTS  (~95 seconds)
========================================================

Now the results — and the main finding of the project lives here.

[point to the letters table — slow down]

Look at the letters. Offline, on the test set, the image model scores a
perfect 100 percent and the landmark model 99.4. So offline, the image
model is the default winner. And recall it ships in 15.71 megabytes,
while the landmark model is just 0.24 — roughly 65 times smaller.

But watch the budget phone — the TECNO. The image model drops to 92.9
percent, while the landmark model stays at 100. The ranking inverts. The
model that looked best in the lab becomes the worse one on a cheap
phone. The reason is that the landmark abstraction discards exactly the
camera noise — white balance, slight blur — that a pixel-based model is
sensitive to.

[point to the words table]

For words, the ensemble reaches 56 percent offline and clears my 40
percent acceptance bar comfortably. On the phone it gets 49 percent
Top-1, and 72 percent Top-3.

Now compare single and ensemble on the phone. The ensemble barely moves
Top-3 — 71 to 72 — but it lifts Top-1 from 38 to 49. So it isn't finding
new answers; it's re-ranking labels the single model already kept inside
its Top-3.

[point to the latency table]

And latency closes the argument. On the flagship Xiaomi, every model
runs at 30 FPS. On the budget TECNO, the word ensemble takes 665
milliseconds per prediction — at that point it's no longer interactive,
which is why I couldn't evaluate the word task on the TECNO at all.

[pause — the punchline]

So the conclusion of the whole project is one sentence: the right model
is not universal — it is device-conditional. On a budget phone, you ship
the landmark model; on a flagship, you can afford the heavier one. That
is the practical guideline this thesis delivers.

Thank you for listening — I'm happy to take any questions.
