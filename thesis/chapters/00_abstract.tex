\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}

This thesis presents an end-to-end American Sign Language (ASL)
recognition system that runs entirely on a consumer Android phone.
Two complementary tasks are addressed: fingerspelling recognition
over the 29 classes of the ASL Alphabet dataset, and word-level
recognition over the 100-class WLASL-100 vocabulary. For the letter
task, two classifiers are trained and compared: an EfficientNet-B0
that consumes raw image crops and a compact multi-layer perceptron
that consumes 21 MediaPipe hand landmarks. They reach
\SI{100}{\percent} and \SI{99.38}{\percent} Top-1 accuracy
respectively on the held-out test partition, with the landmark
model shipping in a \SI{0.24}{MB} checkpoint. For the word task, a
bidirectional LSTM is trained on a two-handed 128-dimensional
landmark feature stream; a five-seed logit-averaging ensemble reaches
\SI{56.22}{\percent} Top-1 and \SI{81.09}{\percent} Top-5 on the
WLASL-100 test partition, clearing the project proposal's hard
acceptance threshold of \SI{40}{\percent} Top-1 by sixteen
percentage points. All four classifiers are exported to TensorFlow
Lite and deployed in an Android application built around CameraX
and MediaPipe Hands. A manual evaluation on two phones spanning
the budget (TECNO SPARK 20C) and flagship (Xiaomi 15T Pro)
segments characterises on-device throughput, latency, and the
single-user out-of-distribution accuracy gap, and produces a
device-conditional model-selection guideline for mobile sign
language deployment.

\vspace{1cm}
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} sign language recognition, ASL, MediaPipe, EfficientNet, BiLSTM, mobile deployment, TensorFlow Lite.
