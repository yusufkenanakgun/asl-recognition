\chapter{Analysis and Design}
\label{ch:analysis-design}

This chapter formalises the recognition problem introduced in
Chapter~\ref{ch:introduction} and sets out the design that
Chapter~\ref{ch:implementation} subsequently realises. It is
deliberately platform- and language-agnostic: it describes what the
system must do and how its components are organised, without
committing to the specific libraries, model versions or training
recipes that the next chapter unpacks. Section~\ref{sec:design-fr}
specifies the functional requirements derived from the project
proposal; Section~\ref{sec:design-nfr} translates the proposal's
evaluation criteria into measurable non-functional requirements;
Sections~\ref{sec:design-usecase}--\ref{sec:design-sequence} document
the system from the user, structural and behavioural viewpoints
respectively; and Section~\ref{sec:design-algorithm} fixes the two
core algorithms---augmented training and ensemble inference---in
pseudo-code so that they can be reproduced without reference to a
particular framework.

%==============================================================================
\section{Functional Requirements}
\label{sec:design-fr}

The project proposal commits to a single high-level capability:
``translates hand gestures into text'' via two complementary
recognition regimes (static fingerspelling and dynamic word-level
signing), evaluated on the ASL Alphabet and WLASL datasets and
delivered as a real-time mobile demo. From this scope statement six
functional requirements are derived. Each one is phrased as an
observable behaviour rather than as an implementation choice; the
mapping from requirement to component is the subject of
Chapter~\ref{ch:implementation}.

\begin{description}
  \item[FR-01 --- Image-based letter recognition.] The system shall
        classify a single RGB image of a hand into one of the 29
        classes of the ASL Alphabet dataset using a convolutional
        neural network. The output shall include the predicted class
        and a confidence score.

  \item[FR-02 --- Landmark-based letter recognition.] The system
        shall provide an alternative letter classifier that consumes
        the 21 hand keypoints extracted by MediaPipe Hands rather
        than the raw image. The classifier shall expose the same
        29-class output interface as FR-01 so that the two pipelines
        can be compared on an equal footing.

  \item[FR-03 --- Word-level recognition.] The system shall
        classify short video clips of isolated ASL signs into one of
        the 100 glosses of the WLASL-100 subset using a temporal
        sequence model over per-frame hand landmarks. The temporal
        model operates on a fixed-length frame window so that its
        inference cost is bounded.

  \item[FR-04 --- On-device mobile demonstration.] The system shall
        host all letter and word classifiers in a mobile application
        that runs on a consumer Android smartphone, captures the
        device camera feed in real time, executes inference on the
        device (no server-side component), and renders the predicted
        sign together with its confidence in the user interface.

  \item[FR-05 --- Comparative evaluation.] The system shall produce
        a reproducible evaluation that reports, for every
        classifier, the metrics specified in the project proposal:
        Top-1 accuracy, Top-5 accuracy, per-class F1 score, confusion
        matrix, frames per second, and serialised model size in
        megabytes.

  \item[FR-06 --- Data and preprocessing pipeline.] The system
        shall implement a deterministic preprocessing pipeline that
        ingests the source datasets, partitions them into training,
        validation and test splits, applies the modality-specific
        feature extraction (image normalisation for FR-01, landmark
        extraction for FR-02 and FR-03), and caches intermediate
        artefacts so that experiments are reproducible from the same
        seed.
\end{description}

a class-diagram-driven object-oriented model of the data flow;
Section~\ref{sec:intro-scope} of Chapter~\ref{ch:introduction}
states the full scope boundary.

\section{Non-Functional Requirements}
\label{sec:design-nfr}

The proposal does not list non-functional requirements as such, but
its evaluation-criteria field commits the system to specific
measurement axes, and its hardware field commits to a consumer-grade
deployment platform. The following non-functional requirements are
the engineering elaboration of those commitments. They are stated as
acceptance properties of the finished system rather than as
optimisation targets, and they are revisited quantitatively in
Chapter~\ref{ch:test-results}.

\begin{description}
  \item[NFR-01 --- Real-time on-device inference.] The end-to-end
        camera-to-prediction pipeline shall sustain a frame rate
        sufficient for an interactive user experience on a
        mid-range Android device, without offloading any inference
        step to a remote server.

  \item[NFR-02 --- Reportable accuracy.] Each classifier shall be
        accompanied by a held-out test-set evaluation that reports
        Top-1 accuracy, Top-5 accuracy (where the number of classes
        makes this informative), per-class F1 score and a confusion
        matrix.

  \item[NFR-03 --- Reproducible training.] Every reported result
        shall be reproducible from a documented combination of
        dataset, random seed and hyperparameter configuration.
        Long-running preprocessing steps shall be cached on disk
        and invalidated only when their inputs change.

  \item[NFR-04 --- Graceful handling of detection failure.] When
        the landmark extractor fails to find a hand in the input
        frame, the system shall communicate this state to the user
        rather than display a previously cached or random
        prediction, and shall not crash.

  \item[NFR-05 --- Deployable model footprint.] The serialised size
        of each classifier checkpoint shall be reported in
        megabytes alongside its accuracy so that the
        accuracy-versus-footprint trade-off can be evaluated. The
        mobile application shall be capable of hosting all
        classifiers within the storage budget of a typical Android
        installable package.

  \item[NFR-06 --- Modular extensibility.] The training and
        inference pipelines shall be parametric in the number of
        output classes so that adding new fingerspelling or word
        classes does not require structural code changes.

  \item[NFR-07 --- Documented dependencies.] All software
        dependencies shall be pinned to specific versions; the
        system shall build and run on a consumer development laptop
        with an NVIDIA GPU, as specified by the proposal hardware
        field.
\end{description}

the measurement instrument that will be used to verify it in
Chapter~\ref{ch:test-results}.

Two non-functional axes that are sometimes treated as requirements
in the broader sign-recognition literature are deliberately omitted
here. The first is a numeric accuracy threshold per task: the
proposal does not pin one, and Chapter~\ref{ch:test-results} reports
the achieved accuracy against published baselines on the same data
rather than against a self-imposed pass/fail bar. The second is a
universal model-size cap: a single cap would penalise the image
network unfairly relative to the landmark network even though both
are useful at different points of the accuracy/footprint curve, and
the comparative framing of the thesis (Chapter~\ref{ch:introduction})
is the more informative axis to report on.

\section{Use Case View}
\label{sec:design-usecase}

The system exposes four end-user use cases, all of them initiated
by the same actor---the \emph{Signer}, who points the device camera
at his or her own hand. Three of the four are recognition cases:
UC-1 and UC-2 cover fingerspelling under the two competing letter
classifiers (image-based EfficientNet-B0 and landmark-based MLP),
and UC-3 covers isolated word recognition with the BiLSTM. The
fourth case, UC-4, lets the user switch the active recognition
mode at runtime; the choice between the single-seed and the
five-seed ensemble word classifier is exposed through this same
toggle rather than as a separate user case, since from the
signer's perspective both produce the same Top-3 prediction
surface. A fifth, supporting case---``handle no-hand
frame''---is included in the model because it is the only
exception path the user can observe from the user interface.

Figure~\ref{fig:design-usecase} shows the resulting use-case
diagram. The notation follows UML~2.5: the rectangular boundary
represents the mobile application; the elliptical nodes are use
cases; and the \textsf{<<extend>>} association captures the
no-hand exception path, which extends the three recognition cases
only when MediaPipe Hands fails to detect a hand in the current
frame.

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/ch3_use_case.pdf}
\caption{Use case diagram of the mobile recognition system. A
single \emph{Signer} actor initiates one of three recognition use
cases (UC-1 image-based letter, UC-2 landmark-based letter, UC-3
word) or switches the active recognition mode (UC-4). All three
recognition use cases are optionally extended by the no-hand
fallback (UC-5) whenever MediaPipe Hands fails to detect a hand
in the current frame.}
\label{fig:design-usecase}
\end{figure}

Three points about the diagram are worth noting because they recur
in the architectural and behavioural views below. First, the
three recognition cases (UC-1, UC-2, UC-3) share the same UI
surface---the camera preview, the Top-3 panel and the FPS/latency
badge described in Section~\ref{sec:impl-android-ui}---and differ
only in which classifier is wired in behind the segmented model
toggle exposed by UC-4. Second, the no-hand fallback is a single
shared exception path: the recovery behaviour (faded prediction
text, no false-positive classifier call) is identical regardless
of which recognition case was active. Third, no administrative or
system-management actor is modelled, because the application has
no remote backend, no user accounts and no settings persistence
beyond the active model selection.

\section{System Architecture}
\label{sec:design-arch}

The system splits cleanly into two physically separate stacks that
share a single artefact: the trained classifier weights. The
\emph{offline training stack} runs on a development workstation and
turns raw datasets into serialised model checkpoints. The
\emph{on-device inference stack} runs on the user's Android phone
and turns live camera frames into on-screen predictions using those
same checkpoints, converted to a mobile inference format.
Figure~\ref{fig:design-architecture} renders the two stacks as a
single block diagram with the checkpoint hand-off marked explicitly.

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/ch3_architecture.pdf}
\caption{End-to-end system architecture. The offline training stack
on a workstation produces TFLite checkpoints; the on-device
inference stack consumes the same checkpoints inside the Android
application.}
\label{fig:design-architecture}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Offline Training Stack}
\label{sec:design-arch-offline}

The training stack is a pure data-flow pipeline. Raw inputs enter
on the left: roughly \num{87000} RGB images from the ASL Alphabet
dataset, and roughly \num{1500} short MP4 clips from the
reconstructed WLASL-100 collection. The preprocessing stage forks
by modality. For the image branch (FR-01) the pipeline resizes
each frame to $224 \times 224$, applies ImageNet normalisation and
emits a tensor batch directly to the EfficientNet trainer. For the
landmark branches (FR-02 and FR-03) MediaPipe Hands is invoked
once per image or per sampled frame, and the resulting
21-keypoint vectors are cached on disk; the same cache is consumed
by both the MLP letter trainer and the BiLSTM word trainer, so the
dominant cost of landmark extraction is paid only once.

Three parallel training routines fan out from the cached features.
Each routine consumes its own dataset slice (a stratified 70/15/15
split for the letter task and the official WLASL-100 split for the
word task), executes the architecture-specific training loop and
writes a single ``best-by-validation'' checkpoint to disk. The
word trainer additionally runs five times with different random
seeds to populate the ensemble. The final stage is a deterministic
PyTorch-to-TFLite converter that takes each checkpoint and emits a
mobile-compatible flatbuffer; the four flatbuffers are then bundled
as assets into the Android application's APK.

\subsection{On-Device Inference Stack}
\label{sec:design-arch-ondevice}

The on-device stack is event-driven rather than data-flow. The
camera library delivers preview frames into an analysis queue at
the camera's native rate. A \emph{FrameAnalyzer} component removes
a frame from the queue, hands it to the MediaPipe landmark engine
in live-stream mode, and maintains a rolling estimate of the
achieved frame rate that the UI badge consumes. The
\emph{InferenceManager} component sits behind a small interface
common to all four classifiers
(\textsf{predict(input)}~$\to$~\textsf{softmaxOutput}) and
dispatches the current landmark or pixel input to whichever model
the user has selected from the on-screen toggle. Its output flows
into a view-model component, which the preview screen observes and
which drives the Top-3 prediction panel.

This separation places three policy boundaries inside the stack
that are otherwise easy to entangle. The camera library is the
only component aware of pixels; the landmark engine is the only
component aware of MediaPipe; the inference runtime is the only
component aware of model weights; and the UI is the only component
aware of human-readable labels. The integration glue between them
is intentionally thin so that swapping any one of the four for a
future replacement (a different camera library, a different
landmark extractor, a different inference runtime or a different
UI framework) does not perturb the other three.
Chapter~\ref{ch:implementation} documents the concrete classes
that implement these boundaries.

\section{Behavioural View}
\label{sec:design-sequence}

The use-case and architectural views describe \emph{what} the
system does and \emph{how} it is structured; the present section
describes \emph{when}. Two sequence diagrams cover the two
recognition regimes and together account for the per-frame
behaviour of every primary use case in
Figure~\ref{fig:design-usecase}, and a state diagram summarises the
application-level lifecycle inside which the per-frame behaviour
takes place.

\subsection{Letter Recognition Flow}
\label{sec:design-sequence-letter}

Letter recognition is a stateless per-frame pipeline: each preview
frame is processed independently, with no buffer or temporal state
maintained between frames.
Figure~\ref{fig:design-sequence-letter} shows the sequence of
messages exchanged between the participants---\textsf{Signer},
camera library, \textsf{FrameAnalyzer}, MediaPipe landmark engine,
\textsf{InferenceManager} and the UI---for a single frame.

\begin{sidewaysfigure}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/ch3_sequence_letter.pdf}
\caption{Sequence diagram of the letter recognition flow for a
single preview frame. The \texttt{alt} fragment distinguishes the
two letter classifiers; the \texttt{opt} fragment captures the
no-hand fallback path.}
\label{fig:design-sequence-letter}
\end{sidewaysfigure}

Three message-passing properties shape the rest of the design.
First, the landmark engine's asynchronous detection call returns
its result on a callback thread; the analyzer must therefore
maintain a strictly monotonic frame timestamp to avoid the
``smaller timestamp than processed timestamp'' exception that the
live-stream API throws on out-of-order inputs
(Section~\ref{sec:impl-android-edges}). Second, the classifier
invocation is conditional on which model the user has selected,
but the two branches share the same downstream messages, which is
why the four primary use cases of Figure~\ref{fig:design-usecase}
produce the same UI behaviour. Third, the no-hand fallback
short-circuits the classifier call entirely: when the landmark
list is empty, the analyzer skips inference rather than invoke
either classifier on a zero vector, which is what NFR-04 requires.

\subsection{Word Recognition Flow}
\label{sec:design-sequence-word}

Word recognition introduces a temporal buffer that letter
recognition does not need. The classifier expects a fixed window
of landmark frames, so a single preview frame is not sufficient to
produce a prediction.
Figure~\ref{fig:design-sequence-word} shows the resulting flow,
which differs from the letter flow in two places: a sliding buffer
is maintained inside the analyzer, and the classifier call is
gated on the buffer being full.

\begin{sidewaysfigure}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/ch3_sequence_word.pdf}
\caption{Sequence diagram of the word recognition flow. The
sliding \texttt{SequenceBuffer} accumulates per-frame features
until the window of 32 frames is full; the ensemble fragment
expands the classifier call into five seeded inferences whose
logits are averaged before softmax.}
\label{fig:design-sequence-word}
\end{sidewaysfigure}

The buffer-fill phase deserves a brief comment. While the buffer
is filling---which takes roughly one second at 30~FPS---the system
shows a sequence-progress overlay rather than a confident
prediction, so that the user receives feedback that the
application is alive without being misled by a low-quality early
classification. Once the buffer is full, the classifier is invoked
on every subsequent frame, so the effective prediction rate
matches the camera frame rate after the initial fill. The ensemble
variant extends the classifier call into a loop over five seeded
models whose logits are averaged before the softmax; this is the
structure that Section~\ref{sec:design-algorithm-ensemble} pins
down in pseudo-code.

\subsection{Application Lifecycle}
\label{sec:design-sequence-state}

The application as a whole moves through a small number of
behavioural states whose transitions are independent of the
recognition task. At launch it is in
\textsf{Requesting~Permission}. Once the camera permission is
granted it transitions to \textsf{Initialising}, during which the
landmark engine and the inference interpreters are loaded on a
background dispatcher; this state is what produces the cold-start
delay observed in Section~\ref{sec:results-on-device}. The
application then settles into \textsf{Streaming}, the steady state
in which all the sequence-diagram traffic of the previous
subsections takes place. Two recoverable sub-states branch off
from \textsf{Streaming}: \textsf{No~Hand~Detected} (entered
whenever the landmark callback returns an empty list and exited as
soon as a hand is detected again) and \textsf{Background}
(entered on \texttt{onPause} when the user leaves the application
and exited on \texttt{onResume}, with the classifier weights
retained in memory so that the second resumption is essentially
instantaneous). Figure~\ref{fig:design-state} summarises the
transitions.

\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/ch3_state.pdf}
\caption{State diagram of the mobile application. The
\texttt{Streaming} state is the steady state in which both

\section{Algorithmic Design}
\label{sec:design-algorithm}

Three algorithms drive most of the system's non-trivial run-time
behaviour and are pinned down here so that they can be reproduced
in a different framework or compared against alternative
formulations. Section~\ref{sec:design-algorithm-normalisation}
fixes the per-frame feature representation that the landmark
pipelines consume;
Section~\ref{sec:design-algorithm-training} fixes the
mixup-augmented training step that the word model relies on; and
Section~\ref{sec:design-algorithm-ensemble} fixes the
logit-averaging inference step that the ensemble variant performs
at run time. The concrete hyperparameter values that populate the
algorithms appear in Chapter~\ref{ch:implementation}.

\subsection{Per-Frame Feature Representation}
\label{sec:design-algorithm-normalisation}

The word classifier consumes a fixed-length sequence of
128-dimensional feature vectors, one per sampled video frame. The
features are constructed in two stages: a per-hand normalisation
that removes signer-to-camera distance variance, and a left/right
channel assignment that turns the landmark engine's two-element
output into a fixed-layout vector.

Per-hand normalisation places the wrist at the origin and uses the
wrist-to-MCP distance as the unit of length:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{c}'_i \;=\;
    \frac{\mathbf{c}_i - \mathbf{c}_0}
         {\lVert \mathbf{c}_9 - \mathbf{c}_0 \rVert},
  \qquad i \in \{0, 1, \dots, 20\},
  \label{eq:design-norm}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{c}_i \in \mathbb{R}^3$ is the $i$-th raw landmark,
$\mathbf{c}_0$ the wrist and $\mathbf{c}_9$ the middle-finger MCP
joint. The resulting normalised coordinates are concatenated with
a binary presence flag $p \in \{0, 1\}$ that drops to zero
whenever the landmark engine fails to detect the hand on the
current frame, yielding a 64-dimensional per-hand block. The left
and right blocks are concatenated in a fixed order (left first,
then right) to produce the 128-dimensional per-frame feature
vector
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{f} \;=\; \bigl[\,
    p_L,\; \mathbf{c}'_{L,0}, \dots, \mathbf{c}'_{L,20},\;
    p_R,\; \mathbf{c}'_{R,0}, \dots, \mathbf{c}'_{R,20}
  \,\bigr]
  \;\in\; \mathbb{R}^{128}.
  \label{eq:design-feature}
\end{equation}
The presence flag is what makes
equation~\eqref{eq:design-feature} unambiguous when a hand is
absent: the corresponding coordinate block is zero-padded and the
network is told, through the flag, to interpret the zeros as ``no
information here'' rather than as a hand at the origin.

\subsection{Mixup-Augmented Training Step}
\label{sec:design-algorithm-training}

The word classifier is trained with a mixup-regularised
cross-entropy loss that linearly interpolates pairs of training
sequences and their labels. Algorithm~\ref{alg:design-train}
states the single training step in framework-independent form; the
outer training loop (initialisation, optimiser update,
learning-rate scheduling and early stopping) is the standard
supervised template and is not reproduced here.

\begin{algorithm}[h]
\caption{Mixup-augmented training step (word classifier).}
\label{alg:design-train}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Batch $(\mathbf{X}, \mathbf{y})$ with
         $\mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times T \times D}$,
         network $f_\theta$, mixup parameter $\alpha$,
         label-smoothing factor $\epsilon$
\State Apply sequence augmentations to $\mathbf{X}$
       \Comment{temporal jitter, frame dropout, Gaussian noise, time-scaling}
\State Sample $\lambda \sim \mathrm{Beta}(\alpha, \alpha)$
\State Sample a random permutation $\sigma$ of $\{1, \dots, B\}$
\State $\tilde{\mathbf{X}} \gets
       \lambda\,\mathbf{X} + (1 - \lambda)\,\mathbf{X}_{\sigma}$
\State $\tilde{\mathbf{p}} \gets
       \mathrm{softmax}(f_\theta(\tilde{\mathbf{X}}))$
\State $\mathcal{L} \gets
       \lambda\, \mathrm{CE}_\epsilon(\tilde{\mathbf{p}}, \mathbf{y})
       + (1 - \lambda)\, \mathrm{CE}_\epsilon(\tilde{\mathbf{p}},
       \mathbf{y}_{\sigma})$
\State Backpropagate $\mathcal{L}$ through $\theta$ and update
       parameters
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

Three modelling choices in Algorithm~\ref{alg:design-train} are
worth flagging because they directly affect the empirical results
of Chapter~\ref{ch:test-results}. Sequence augmentations are
applied \emph{before} the mixup interpolation, not after, so that
the interpolation operates on already-perturbed inputs rather than
on two clean clips. Label smoothing is applied inside each
cross-entropy term separately rather than once over the mixed
target, which yields the same gradient but is numerically more
stable when $\lambda$ is close to one. The mixup coefficient is
drawn per batch rather than per sample, following common practice;
this trades a small amount of in-batch decorrelation for a much
simpler implementation, with no measurable accuracy cost in the
ablation of Section~\ref{sec:results-ablation}.

\subsection{Ensemble Inference}
\label{sec:design-algorithm-ensemble}

The five-seed ensemble averages the pre-softmax logits of its
constituent models rather than averaging their softmax outputs.
Algorithm~\ref{alg:design-ensemble} states the inference step that
the mobile demo invokes whenever the user has selected the
ensemble variant from the model toggle.

\begin{algorithm}[h]
\caption{Logit-averaged ensemble inference (word classifier).}
\label{alg:design-ensemble}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Input sequence
         $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times D}$,
         $N$ trained networks
         $f_{\theta_1}, \dots, f_{\theta_N}$
\State $\boldsymbol\ell_{\text{ens}} \gets
       \mathbf{0} \in \mathbb{R}^{C}$
\For{$n \gets 1 \;\textbf{to}\; N$}
  \State $\boldsymbol\ell_n \gets f_{\theta_n}(\mathbf{x})$
         \Comment{forward pass of the $n$-th seed}
  \State $\boldsymbol\ell_{\text{ens}} \gets
         \boldsymbol\ell_{\text{ens}} + \boldsymbol\ell_n$
\EndFor
\State $\boldsymbol\ell_{\text{ens}} \gets
       \boldsymbol\ell_{\text{ens}} / N$
\State $\mathbf{p} \gets
       \mathrm{softmax}(\boldsymbol\ell_{\text{ens}})$
\State \Return $\arg\max_c \mathbf{p}_c$ and the top-three
       $\mathbf{p}_c$ for UI display
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

Averaging logits rather than probabilities preserves the relative
magnitudes of each network's confidence and is the standard choice
for small ensembles in the
literature~\cite{dietterich2000ensemble}. The mobile
implementation runs the $N$ forward passes sequentially on a
single inference runtime, which means the end-to-end latency of
the ensemble call is approximately $N$ times the latency of a
single forward pass; Section~\ref{sec:results-on-device}
quantifies this overhead on the test device.

\section{Summary}
\label{sec:design-summary}

This chapter has converted the project proposal into a small set
of functional and non-functional requirements, has presented the
system from the user, structural and behavioural viewpoints, and
has pinned down the three algorithmic primitives that drive most
of the non-trivial run-time behaviour. The next chapter takes each
of these design elements and documents the concrete components,
libraries and hyperparameters that realise them on the development
laptop and on the Android test device. Wherever the implementation
deviates from the design as stated here---most notably in the
choice of a native Kotlin mobile stack in place of the React
Native stack suggested by the proposal---the deviation is recorded
explicitly in Chapter~\ref{ch:implementation} together with the
engineering reason for the change.
